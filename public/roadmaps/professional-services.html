Privacy Compliance Roadmap

Professional Services (Accountants, Lawyers, Consultants, Advisors) · Privacy Act 1988 · 13 Australian Privacy Principles

Professional services handle sensitive client information. You likely collect financial records, legal documents, tax file numbers, and other sensitive data. This roadmap addresses the higher compliance bar that applies to your sector.

Your 6-Week Implementation Plan

Professional services firms face stricter obligations because of the volume and sensitivity of client data they handle. This roadmap prioritises the areas that matter most for your sector.

Week 1 Publish your privacy policy and add privacy collection notices to client intake forms and engagement letters.
Week 2 Audit client data flows. Map every system that holds client information, from practice management to cloud storage to email.
Week 3 Secure sensitive data. Review access controls, encryption, and file-sharing practices for client files.
Week 4 Document data handling procedures covering client file management, retention, destruction, and cross-border transfers.
Week 5 Prepare breach response plan. Professional services breaches can trigger professional body notification requirements too.
Week 6 Train staff and review engagement letters. Ensure privacy obligations are embedded in how you onboard clients.

Step 1: Client Data Audit

Professional services firms typically hold data across multiple systems. The audit needs to cover all of them.

Checklist

  • List all systems holding client data: practice management software, email, cloud storage, accounting software, CRM
  • Document what personal information each system holds (names, addresses, TFNs, financial records, legal documents)
  • Identify sensitive information you collect: tax file numbers, financial records, health information, government IDs
  • Map data flows: how does client information move between systems? Who has access at each stage?
  • Check third-party integrations: does your practice management software share data with other services?
  • Review client portal security if you use one (access controls, session timeouts, encryption)
  • Audit paper records: where are physical client files stored? Who has access?
  • Document any personal information shared with subcontractors, referral partners, or offshore service providers

Step 2: Client-Facing Privacy Notices

APP 5 requires you to notify individuals about how you'll handle their information at or before the time of collection. For professional services, this means updating your client-facing documents.

Checklist

  • Add a privacy collection notice to your engagement letter or terms of engagement
  • Include a privacy notice on client intake forms (paper and digital)
  • Update your website forms with a link to your privacy policy
  • If you collect TFNs or other government identifiers, add specific notices about how these are handled (APP 9)
  • If you share client information with third parties (e.g., ATO, ASIC, referral partners), disclose this upfront
  • Review your email signature and standard correspondence for privacy notice links

Step 3: Security for Sensitive Data

Professional services firms hold some of the most sensitive personal information. APP 11 requires reasonable security measures proportionate to the sensitivity of the data.

Checklist

  • Enable two-factor authentication on all systems holding client data (practice management, email, cloud storage)
  • Use encrypted file sharing for client documents (not plain email attachments for sensitive files)
  • Set up role-based access: junior staff should not have access to all client files
  • Encrypt laptops and mobile devices that access client data
  • Use a password manager for the practice (no shared passwords on sticky notes)
  • Implement automatic screen lock on all workstations (5-minute timeout)
  • Review and restrict USB drive and external storage access
  • Secure physical office: locked filing cabinets, clean desk policy, visitor sign-in
  • Set up automated backups of client data and test restore procedures quarterly
  • If using cloud storage, confirm data is stored in Australia or a jurisdiction with adequate privacy protections (APP 8)

Step 4: Data Handling Procedures

These are the operational rules your practice follows for managing client information throughout its lifecycle.

Checklist

  • Document your client file lifecycle: creation, active use, archive, destruction
  • Set retention periods aligned with professional body requirements (typically 7 years for accounting, varies for legal)
  • Document your secure destruction method (cross-cut shredding for paper, secure deletion for digital)
  • Create a process for handling client access requests: who responds, within what timeframe (30 days under APP 12)
  • Create a process for handling correction requests (APP 13)
  • Document how you handle subpoenas and legal requests for client information
  • If you use direct marketing (newsletters, updates), document opt-out procedures (APP 7)
  • If you transfer data overseas (including to cloud services hosted internationally), document the countries and safeguards (APP 8)

Step 5: Breach Response Plan

A data breach involving client financial records, TFNs, or legal documents can cause serious harm. Your response plan needs to be ready before an incident occurs.

Checklist

  • Designate a breach response lead (partner or principal)
  • Document the response steps: contain the breach, assess the scope, notify if required
  • Know the Notifiable Data Breaches threshold: notify OAIC and affected individuals if the breach is likely to cause serious harm
  • Check your professional body's breach notification requirements (may be additional to OAIC requirements)
  • Check your professional indemnity insurance coverage for data breach incidents
  • Prepare template notifications for: OAIC, affected clients, professional body
  • Document forensic preservation steps (don't delete evidence while investigating)
  • Test the plan with a tabletop exercise: "A staff member's laptop with client tax returns is stolen from their car"

Step 6: Staff Training and Engagement Letters

Staff Training Checklist

  • Brief all staff on the privacy policy and data handling procedures
  • Cover specific risks for your practice: email misdirection, accidental disclosure, social engineering
  • Train staff on recognising and reporting suspected breaches
  • Include privacy compliance in onboarding for new staff and contractors
  • Schedule annual refresher training (document dates and attendance)
  • Include privacy obligations in employment contracts and contractor agreements

Engagement Letter Updates

  • Add a privacy clause to your standard engagement letter template
  • Specify what personal information you'll collect and why
  • Disclose third parties who will receive client information (e.g., ATO for tax agents)
  • Include a reference to your full privacy policy
  • State how clients can access, correct, or complain about their information

Key date: From 1 July 2026, the small business exemption is being removed for AML-CTF reporting entities. Most accounting firms and many legal practices are AML-CTF reporting entities. If you haven't already, confirm your status and ensure full compliance by this date.

Professional body requirements: Your professional body (CPA Australia, CA ANZ, Law Society, etc.) may have additional privacy and data handling requirements beyond the Privacy Act. Check their guidance and ensure your procedures meet both sets of obligations.
