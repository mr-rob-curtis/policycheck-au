## Stimulus Tested

**Product:** PolicyCheck AU - Automated privacy compliance tool for Australian small businesses

**Scenario:** Cold email outreach from Artemis:
"We've reviewed your privacy policy and found it non-compliant with Australia's new Privacy Act (effective July 2026). Your compliance score: 31/100. See the biggest gap here [1 detailed gap]. Full analysis available [paywall]."

**Value proposition:** 
- Scans website, finds or flags absence of privacy policy
- Analyses against 13 Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)
- Produces score (0-100) and gap analysis
- Generates recommended policy language
- Delivers ready-to-paste HTML snippet

**Pricing:** Free (score + 1 gap) | A$149 (full analysis + templates) | A$499 (full analysis + policy draft + December 2026 ADM audit)

## Flags for Downstream Skills

### For Stage 5 (Scenario Layer):
- **Time sensitivity finding:** David and Frank (comprehension barrier) may respond differently if the Privacy Act deadline is closer (urgency might overcome confusion). This should be tested in scenario testing with "30 days before deadline vs. 90 days before."
- **Relationship channel finding:** Marcus, James, Lisa all mentioned deferring to trusted advisors (accountants, existing vendors, partners). The scenario layer should test "Does the message land differently if it comes from a trusted source (accountant, professional body) vs. cold email?"
- **Sector-specific messaging:** Tanya's uncertainty about whether Privacy Act applies to dentists suggests that sector-specific messaging ("For dental practices") would significantly improve comprehension. Scenario layer should test sector-specific vs. generic messaging.

### For Stage 6 (Focus Group Debate):
- **Primary debate:** Priya (enthusiast, sees consulting value) vs. Elena (hostile rejector, sees predatory cold email). This is the strongest polarization on the panel. Their exchange will surface the fundamental tension: is this a helpful tool for business owners who need guidance, or a fear-based sales approach exploiting regulatory uncertainty? This debate should be moderated carefully to extract the real insight (which it is, and to whom).
- **Secondary debate:** Sarah (too knowledgeable to need tool) vs. Lisa (aspiring owner who needs guidance). Same product, opposite relevance depending on customer lifecycle stage. This will surface how different audience segments evaluate the same product very differently.
- **Tertiary debate:** Marcus (pragmatist, would use accountant) vs. Amita (specialist, sees consulting opportunity). Both interested, but for completely different reasons. This will surface whether the product's value proposition is "solve your own compliance" or "understand your compliance gaps so you can advise clients."
- **Supporting voices:** Frank, David, Tanya should be invited to represent the "confused/non-believer" position and challenge the enthusiasts to articulate why anyone should care about privacy compliance in sectors they work in.

### For Stage 7 (Research Synthesis):
- **Confidence flags:** 
  - **High confidence:** Cold email conversion rates will be ~40% (strong consistency with 2/3 personas in consistency check holding position across framings)
  - **Medium confidence:** Sector-specific barriers (Tanya's dental uncertainty, David's car sales confusion). These may be addressable with better targeting, or they may indicate the product doesn't work well for less-regulated sectors
  - **Medium confidence:** Price point perception. Only one persona (Elena) explicitly rejected on price grounds. Most rejections were on relevance/credibility grounds. This suggests price may not be the primary optimization variable
  - **High confidence:** Cold email to regulated professions (pharmacy, law) will see higher resistance due to existing compliance relationships and vendor fatigue
- **Recommended analysis priorities:** (1) Identify which sectors convert best (real estate + accounting look strong; pharmacy + car sales look weak), (2) Quantify the impact of source credibility (cold email vs. professional recommendation), (3) Test whether sector-specific messaging solves the comprehension barrier
- **Risk to flag:** The strongest converters (Priya, Amita) are both in specialist/consulting roles, not general small business owners. Make sure the business model assumption (direct-to-business sales via cold email) doesn't depend entirely on converting these specialist segments. If primary revenue comes from advisors using it for clients, distribution and positioning strategy will be very different.
